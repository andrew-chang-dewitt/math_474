## Problem 1

:::{.question}

> (Exercise 2.1.17). Four bits are transmitted over a digital
> communications channel. Each bit is either distorted or received
> without distortion. Let $A_i$ denote the event that the $i^\text{th}$ bit is
> distorted, $i = 1,...,4$.

:::

<section>

### part (a)

:::{.question}

> Describe the sample space for this experiment in two ways, first as a set, and second as a tree diagram.

:::

as a set:

$$
\begin{aligned}
d &\coloneqq \text{distorted bit} \\
n &\coloneqq \text{not distorted bit} \\
S &= \begin{aligned}
       \{
        & dddd, dddn, ddnd, dndd, nddd, ddnn, nndd, dnnd, \\
        & nddn, ndnd, dndn, nnnd, nndn, ndnn, dnnn, nnnn
       \}
     \end{aligned} \\
\end{aligned}
$$

as a tree diagram:

```no-linenums
i
                                          .
1                                       d/ \n
                        -----------------   -----------------
                       /                                     \
                      .                                       .
1                   d/ \n                                   d/ \n
              -------   -------                       -------   -------
             /                 \                     /                 \
            .                   .                   .                   .

         --   --             --   --             --   --             --   --
        /       \           /       \           /       \           /       \
       .         .         .         .         .         .         .         .

     /   \     /   \     /   \     /   \     /   \     /   \     /   \     /   \
   dddd dddn ddnd ddnn dndd dndn dnnd dnnn nddd nddn ndnd ndnn nndd nndn nnnd nnnn
```

</section>
<section>

### part (b)

:::{.question}

> Are the events $A_1$, $A_2$, $A_3$, $A_4$ mutually exclusive?

:::

no, for example consider $A_1$ & $A_2$:

$$
\begin{aligned}
A_1 &= \{ dddd, dddn, ddnd, dndd, ddnn, dnnd, dndn, dnnn \} && \\
A_2 &= \{ dddd, dddn, ddnd, nddd, ddnn, nddn, ndnd, ndnn \} && \\
\end{aligned}
$$

as we can see these two are _**not**_ mutually exclusive,

$$
\begin{alignedat}{2}
\neg \text{MutuallyExclusive}(A_1, A_2)
     &\iff A_1 ∩ A_2 &\neq \empty \\
     &\iff \{ dddd, dddn, ddnd, ddnn \} &\neq \empty \\
     &\iff T \space_\blacksquare
\end{alignedat}
$$

thus $A_1 ∩ A_2 ∩ A_3 ∩ A_4 \neq \empty$, meaning
that $A_1$, $A_2$, $A_3$, $A_4$ are not mutually exclusive as well.

</section>
<section>

### part (c)

:::{.question}

> Describe the outcomes in the event $A_1$.

:::

For all events in $A_1$, the first bit is distorted & the other three could be either distorted or not distorted.

</section>
<section>

### part (d)

:::{.question}

> Describe the outcomes in the event $A′_1$.

:::

For all events in $A'_1$, the first bit is _**not**_ distorted & the other three bits could be either distorted or not distorted.

</section>
<section>

### part (e)

:::{.question}

> Describe the outcomes in the event $A_1 ∩ A_2 ∩ A_3 ∩ A_4$.

:::

Because events $A_i$ are the outcomes such that the $i_\text{ith}$ bit is distorted (`d`), $A_1 ∩ A_2 ∩ A_3 ∩ A_4$ is the event that each bit is distorted, e.g.:

$$
\begin{aligned}
A_1 ∩ A_2 ∩ A_3 ∩ A_4 &= \{ (b_1, b_2, b_3, b_4) \in S \mid b_i = d \; \forall i \in (1,2,3,4) \} \\
                               &= \{ dddd \} \space_\blacksquare
\end{aligned}
$$

</section>
<section>

### part (f)

:::{.question}

> Describe the outcomes in the event $(A′_1 ∩ A_2) ∪ (A_3 ∩ A_4)$.

:::

Using the same definitions of $A_i$ from
above, $(A'_1 ∩ A_2) ∪ (A_3 ∩ A_4)$ is the event that the first bit
is _not_ distorted _and_ that the second bit _is_ distorted _**or**_
the that the third _and_ fourth bits are distorted, e.g.:

$$
\begin{aligned}
(A′_1 ∩ A_2) ∪ (A_3 ∩ A_4) &= \{ (b_1, b_2, b_3, b_4) \in S \\
                           &\quad\quad\quad \mid ( b_1 = n \land b_2 ) = d \lor ( b_3 = b_4 = d ) \} \\
                           &= \begin{aligned}\{
                                & nddd, nddn, ndnd, ndnn, \\
                                & dddd, dndd, nddd, nndd
                              \}\end{aligned} \space_\blacksquare \\
\end{aligned}
$$

## Problem 2

> (Exercise 2.1.14 modified). In an injection-molding operation, the
> length $X$ and width $Y$ (in centimeters) of each molded part are
> measured. A part is said to meet the length specification

>
> $$
> \begin{aligned}
> A &=\{48<X <52\}, \\
> B &=\{9<Y <11\}.
> \end{aligned}
> $$
>
> In a quality audit, 200 parts are inspected. Among them, 170 meet the
> length specification, 150 meet the width specification, and 140 meet
> both specifications.

:::

<section>

### part (a)

:::{.question}

> Describe a reasonable sample space for the outcome of $(X, Y)$.

:::

While a naive sample space may be defined with pairs of real numbers
for $X$ & $Y$ (e.g. $\{(X, Y) \forall X \in \real, Y \in \real \}$
), it is easier to think of it in terms of the events $A$ & $B$ above.
This instead gives outcomes composed of pairs of booleans, e.g.:

$$
\begin{aligned}
S &\coloneqq \{ (X, Y) \forall X \in \text{boolean}, Y \in \text{boolean} \} \\
  &= \{ (T, T), (T, F), (F, T), (F, F) \} \\
\end{aligned}
$$

</section>
<section>

### part (b)

:::{.question}

> Using the information above, complete the following table of counts:

|       | B   | B′  | Total |

| A′    | 10  | 20  | 30    |
| Total | 150 | 50  | 200   |

</section>

### part (c)

:::{.question}

> Convert your table in part (b) into a table of probabilities. Using
> this table, compute $P(A), P(B), P(A∩B), P(A′∩B′), P(A∪B)$

:::

$$
\begin{aligned}
      P(A) &= \frac{170}{200} = 0.85 \\
      P(B) &= \frac{150}{200} = 0.75 \\
  P(A ∩ B) &= \frac{140}{200} = 0.70 \\
P(A' ∩ B') &= \frac{20}{200} = 0.10 \\
  P(A ∪ B) &= \frac{140 + 10 + 30}{200} = \frac{180}{200} = 0.90 \\
\end{aligned}
$$

</section>
<section>

### part (d)

:::{.question}

> Use the formula for the probability of a union to compute $P(A ∪ B)$

:::

$$
\begin{aligned}
P(A ∪ B) &= P(A) + P(B) - P(A \cap B) \\
         &= \frac{140}{200} + \frac{10}{200} + \frac{30}{200} \\
         &= 0.90 \\
\end{aligned}
$$

</section>

## Problem 3

:::{.question}

> (Exercise 2.2.7). A batch of 140 semiconductor chips is inspected by
> choosing a sample of 5 chips without replacement. Assume 10 of the
> chips do not conform to customer requirements.

:::

<section>

### part (a)

:::{.question}

> How many different samples are possible?

:::

$$
\begin{aligned}
|S| &= C^n_r && \textit{order chosen doesn't matter} \\
    &= \begin{pmatrix} n \\ r \end{pmatrix} = \frac{n!}{r!(n - r)!} \\
    &= \frac{140!}{5!135!} = \frac{140 \cdot 139 \cdot ... \cdot 136}{5!} \\
|S| &= 416\,965\,528 \space_\blacksquare
\end{aligned}
$$

</section>
<section>

### part (b)

:::{.question}

> How many samples contain exactly one nonconforming chip?

:::

$$
\begin{aligned}
A &\coloneqq \text{event of exactly one nonconforming chip} \\
|A| &= C^{10}_1 \cdot  C^{130}_{4} \\
    &= 10 \cdot \frac{130!}{4!126!} \\
    &= 10 \cdot 11\,358\,880 \\
|A| &= 113\,588\,800 \space_\blacksquare
\end{aligned}
$$

</section>
<section>

### part (c)

:::{.question}

> How many samples contain at least one nonconforming chip?

:::

$$
\begin{aligned}
  B &\coloneqq \text{set of samples w/ one or more nonconforming} \\
|B| &= |S| - C^{130}_5 && \textit{same as $S \ \text{event all conforming}$} \\
    &= |S| - \frac{130!}{5!125!} \\
    &= 416\,965\,528 - 286\,243\,776 \\
|B| &= 130\,721\,752 \space_\blacksquare
\end{aligned}
$$

</section>

## Problem 5

:::{.question}

> (Exercise 2.3.17 and 2.4.9). A computer system uses passwords that
> contain exactly eight characters, and each character is one of 26
> lowercase letters (a–z), 26 uppercase letters (A–Z), or 10 integers
> (0–9). Let $\Omega$ denote the set of all possible passwords, and let $A$
> and $B$ denote the events that consist of passwords with only letters
> or only integers, respectively. Suppose that all passwords in $\Omega$ are
> equally likely. Determine the probability of each of the following:

:::

$$
\begin{aligned}
C &\coloneqq \{ a,b,...,z,A,B,...,Z,0,1,...,9 \} && \textit{set of allowable characters} \\
|C| &= 26 + 26 + 10 = 62 \\
\Omega &\coloneqq \{ c_i \in C \mid \forall i \in [1,8] \} && \textit{set of allowable 8 character passwords} \\
|\Omega| &= 62^8 = 218\,340\,105\,584\,896
\end{aligned}
$$

<section>

### part (a)

:::{.question}

> $A$, event password contains only letters

:::

$$
\begin{aligned}
C_a &\coloneqq \{ a,b,...,z,A,B,...,Z \} \\
A &\coloneqq \{ c_i \in C_a \mid \forall i \in [1,8] \} \\
|A| &= |C_a|^8 = 52^8 = 53\,459\,728\,531\,456 && \textit{by the multiplication rule} \\
\\
P(A) &= \frac{|A|}{|S|} = \frac{53\,459\,728\,531\,456}{218\,340\,105\,584\,896} \\
     &\approx 0.248 \space_\blacksquare
\end{aligned}
$$

</section>
<section>

### part (b)

:::{.question}

> $B$, event password contains only integers

:::

$$
\begin{aligned}
C_b &\coloneqq \{ 0,1,...,9 \} \\
B &\coloneqq \{ c_i \in C_b \mid \forall i \in [1,8] \} \\
|B| &= |C_b|^8 = 10^8 = 100\,000\,000 && \textit{by the multiplication rule} \\
\\
P(B) &= \frac{|B|}{|S|} = \frac{100\,000\,000}{218\,340\,105\,584\,896} \\
     &\approx 0.000000458 \space_\blacksquare
\end{aligned}
$$

</section>
<section>

### part (c)

:::{.question}

> $A ∪ B$

:::

$$
\begin{aligned}
\because
 && C_a \cap C_b &= \empty \implies A \cap B = \empty \\
 &&  P(A \cup B) &= P(A) + P(B) - P(A \cap B) && \textit{addition rule} \\
\\
 &&  P(A \cup B) &= P(A) + P(B) - P(\empty) \\
 &&              &\approx 0.248 + 0.000000458 - 0 \\
 &&              &\approx 0.248000458 \\
\end{aligned}
$$

</section>
<section>

### part (d)

:::{.question}

> $A′ ∪ B$

:::

$$
\begin{align}
\because
 &&          C_a' &\coloneqq C \setminus C_a \implies C_b \subset C_a' \\
 &&            A' &= \Omega \setminus A \\
 && (1) \land (2) &\implies B \subset A' \\
 &&           (3) &\implies A' \cup B = A' \\
\notag \\
 &&  P(A' \cup B) &= P(A') && \textit{by 4, above}  \\
 &&               &= P(\Omega) - P(A) && \textit{by axiom 1} \notag \\
 &&               &\approx 1 - 0.248 \notag \\
 &&  P(A' \cup B) &\approx 0.752 \\
\end{align}
$$

</section>
<section>

### part (e)

:::{.question}

> A password contains at least one integer.

:::

$$
\begin{aligned}
  E &\coloneqq \text{event password contains at least one integer} \\
  E &= \Omega \setminus A = A' \\
\\
P(E) &= P(A') = P(A' \cup B) && \textit{by 5, part d} \\
P(E) &\approx 0.752 \space_\blacksquare && \textit{by 6, part d}
\end{aligned}
$$

</section>
<section>

### part (f)

:::{.question}

> A password contains exactly two integers.

:::

$$
\begin{align}
  F &\coloneqq \text{event password contains exactly two integers} \\
|F| &= 10^2 \cdot 52^6 \notag \\
|F| &= 1\,977\,060\,966\,400 \notag \\
\notag \\
P(F) &= \frac{|F|}{|\Omega|} \notag \\
     &= \frac{1\,977\,060\,966\,400}{218\,340\,105\,584\,896} \notag \\
P(F) &\approx 0.00905 \space_\blacksquare
\end{align}
$$

</section>
<section>

### part (g)

:::{.question}

> A password contains exactly one or two integers.

:::

$$
\begin{aligned}
G &\coloneqq \text{event password contains exactly one or two inteers} \\
H &\coloneqq \text{event password contains exactly one character} \\
G &= F \cup H && \textit{$F$ from 7, part f} \\
F \cap H &= \empty \\
\\
P(G) &= P(F \cup H) = P(F) + P(H) - P(F \cap H) && \textit{by the addition rule} \\
\\
|H| &= 10 \cdot 52^7 = 10\,280\,717\,025\,280 \\
P(H) &= \frac{|H|}{|\Omega|} = \frac{10\,280\,717\,025\,280}{218\,340\,105\,584\,896} \\
\\
P(G) &\approx 0.00905 + 0.00471 \approx 0.0138 \space_\blacksquare && \textit{by 8, part f}
\end{aligned}
$$

</section>
