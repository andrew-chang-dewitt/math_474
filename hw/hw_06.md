## Problem 1

:::{.question}

> (Exercise 4.10.48). The phone lines to an airline reservation system are
> occupied p = 40 % of the time. Assume that the events that the lines are occupied on
> successive calls are independent. Assume that 10 calls are placed to the airline.

:::

This can be modeled as a random variable, $X$, w/ binomial distribution, where a successful trial means "the lines are occupied" with $p = 0.40$ & the number of trials, $n = 10$:

$$
\begin{aligned}
&X \coloneqq \text{number of calls with all lines occupied} \\
&\mathbb{P}(X = x) = \begin{pmatrix}10 \\ x \end{pmatrix} 0.40^x \cdot 0.60^{10 - x} &&
    \htmlClass{hljs-comment}{\textit{// binomial distribution pmf}} \tag{1.1} \\
\end{aligned}
$$

<section>

### part (a)

:::{.question}

> What is the probability that for exactly three calls, the lines are occupied?

:::

$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathbb{P}(X = 3) &= \begin{pmatrix}10 \\ 3 \end{pmatrix} 0.40^3 \cdot 0.60^{10 - 3} &&
    \htmlClass{hljs-comment}{\textit{// by (1.1)}} \\
         &= \frac{10!}{3!(10 - 3)!} 0.40^3 \cdot 0.60^{7} \\
         &= \frac{10\cdot9\cdot8}{3\cdot2} 0.40^3 \cdot 0.60^{7} \\
         &= (10\cdot3\cdot4) \cdot 0.40^3 \cdot 0.60^{7} \\
         &= 120 \cdot 0.064^3 \cdot 0.0279936 \\
\mathbb{P}(X = 3) &\approx 0.215 \space_\blacksquare \\
\end{aligned}
$$

</section>
<section>

### part (b)

:::{.question}

> What is the probability that for at least one call, the lines are not
> occupied?

:::

Let the event that for at least one call the lines are not occupied be $A$, then the complement, $A'$ is the event that for _all calls_ the lines are occupied:

$$
\begin{aligned}
 \mathbb{P}(A) &= 1 - \mathbb{P}(A') \\
\mathbb{P}(A') &= p^{10} = 0.40^{10} = 0.0001048576 \\
 \mathbb{P}(A) &= 1 - 0.0001048576 = 0.9998951424 \space_\blacksquare
\end{aligned}
$$

</section>
<section>

### part (c)

:::{.question}

> What is the expected number of calls in which the lines are occupied?

:::

$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathbb{E}[X] &= np = 10 \cdot 0.40 = 4 \space_\blacksquare &&
    \htmlClass{hljs-comment}{\textit{// by mean of binomial distribution}} \\
\end{aligned}
$$

</section>
</section>

## Problem 2

:::{.question}

> (Exercise 4.10.53). Samples of 20 parts from a metal punching process are
> selected every hour. Typically, p = 1% of the parts require rework. Let X
> denote the number of parts in the sample of 20 that require rework. A process
> problem is suspected if X exceeds its mean by more than 3 standard
> deviations.

:::

Also modeled as a rand var $X$ w/ binomial distribution, where:

$$
\begin{aligned}
&n \coloneqq 20, p \coloneqq 0.01 \\
&\mathbb{P}(X = x) = \begin{pmatrix}20 \\ x \end{pmatrix} 0.01^x \cdot 0.99^{20 - x} &&
    \htmlClass{hljs-comment}{\textit{// binomial distribution pmf}} \tag{2.1} \\
\end{aligned}
$$

<section>

### part (a)

:::{.question}

> If the percentage of parts that require rework remains at p = 1% , what is
> the probability that X exceeds its mean by more than 3 standard deviations?

:::

First, find the mean, $\mathbb{E}[X]$, then the standard deviation, $\sigma$, & finally the value at which $x \in X$ is $> 3 \sigma$.

$$
\begin{aligned}
\mu    &= \mathbb{E}[X] np = 20 \cdot 0.01 = 0.02 \\
\sigma &= \sqrt{\text{Var}[X]} \sqrt{np(1 - p)} \\
       &= \sqrt{0.02(0.99)} = \sqrt{0.198} \\
       &= 0.445 \\
\\
\mathbb{P}(X > \mu + 3\sigma) &= \mathbb{P}(X \gt 2) &&
    \htmlClass{hljs-comment}{\textit{// $\mu + 3\sigma \approx 1.535$ \& $X$ is an integer}} \\
                              &= \mathbb{P}(X \gt 0 \setminus (X = 0 \cup X = 1)) \\
                              &= 1 - \mathbb{P}(X = 0) - \mathbb{P}(X = 1) \\
                              &= 1 - \begin{pmatrix}20 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} 0.01^0 \cdot 0.99^{20 - 0} - \begin{pmatrix}20 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} 0.01^1 \cdot 0.99^{20 - 1} \\
                              &= 1 - 1 \cdot 0.99^{20} - 20 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.99^{19} \\
                              &= 1 - 0.99^{20} - 0.2 \cdot 0.99^{19} \\
                              &\approx 1 - 0.8179 - 0.1652 \\
\mathbb{P}(X > \mu + 3\sigma) &\approx 0.0169 \space_\blacksquare
\end{aligned}
$$

</section>
<section>

### part (b)

:::{.question}

> If the rework percentage increases to p1 = 4 % , what is the probability that
> X exceeds 1?

:::

replace $p$ w/ $p_1$ in $\mathbb{P}(X = x)$:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathbb{P}_1(X = x) &= \begin{pmatrix}20 \\ x \end{pmatrix} 0.04^x \cdot 0.96^{20 - x} &&
    \htmlClass{hljs-comment}{\textit{// by (2.1)}} \\
\\
\mathbb{P}_1(X > 1) &= 1 - \mathbb{P}_1(X = 0) - \mathbb{P}_1(X = 1) \\
                      &= 1 - \begin{pmatrix}20 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} 0.04^0 \cdot 0.96^{20 - 0} - \begin{pmatrix}20 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} 0.04^1 \cdot 0.96^{20 - 1} \\
                      &= 1 - 0.96^{20} - 20 \cdot 0.04 \cdot 0.96^19 \\
\mathbb{P}_1(X > 1) &\approx 0.1896 \space_\blacksquare \\
\end{aligned}
$$

</section>
<section>

### part (c)

:::{.question}

> If the rework percentage increases to p1 = 4 %, what is the probability that
> X exceeds 1 in at least one of the next five hours of samples?

:::

From part (b), above, we know the probability that $X > 1$ for any 1 hour.
Assuming the samples taken in each hour are independent, then the
probability across any 5 hours can be found by the multiplication rule. Finally, the total probability rule tells us that the sample space minus the probability that $X \lt 1$ for all 5 hours is the same thing as the probability $X > 1$ for at least one hour.

$$
\begin{aligned}
    A_i &\coloneqq \text{$X > 1$ in hour $i$} \\
   A'_i &\coloneqq \text{$X \lt 1$ in hour $i$} \\
\mathbb{P}(A'_i) &= 1 - \mathbb{P}(A_i) \approx 1 - 0.1896 = 0.8104 &&
      \htmlClass{hljs-comment}{\textit{// by part (b)}} \\
\\
\mathbb{P}(\text{at least 1 hour $X > 1$}) &= 1 - \mathbb{P}(\text{no hour $X > 1$}) \\
                                  &= 1 - \mathbb{P}(A'_i)^5 = 1 - 0.8104^5 \\
\mathbb{P}(\text{at least 1 hour $X > 1$}) &\approx 0.65 \space_\blacksquare
\end{aligned}
$$

</section>
</section>

## Problem 3

:::{.question}

The analysis of results from a
leaf transmutation experiment (turning a leaf into a petal) is
summarized by the type of transformation completed:

|                          | Textural Transformation Yes | Textural Transformation No | Total |
| ------------------------ | --------------------------- | -------------------------- | ----- |
| Color Transformation Yes | 243                         | 26                         | 269   |
| Color Transformation No  | 13                          | 18                         | 31    |
| Total                    | 256                         | 44                         | 300   |

A naturalist randomly selects three leaves from this set without
replacement. Determine the following probabilities.

:::

$$
\begin{aligned}
&T \coloneqq \text{1 leaf textural transformation}, C \coloneqq \text{1 leaf color transformation} \\
&\mathbb{P}(T) = \frac{256}{300} \approx 0.853 \\
&\mathbb{P}(C) = \frac{269}{300} \approx 0.897 \\
&\mathbb{P}(T \cap C) = \frac{243}{300} \approx 0.81 \\
\end{aligned}
$$

<section>

### part (a)

:::{.question}

> Exactly one has undergone both types of transformations.

:::

This is a combination where 1 of 3 is event $T \cap C$ (243 of 300) & the other two are from the remaining 57.

$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathbb{P} &= \frac{\begin{pmatrix} 243 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 57 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix}}{\begin{pmatrix} 300 \\ 3 \end{pmatrix}} \\
  &= \frac{243 \cdot 1596}{4455100} \\
  &\approx 0.0872 \space_\blacksquare
\end{aligned}
$$

</section>
<section>

### part (b)

:::{.question}

> Exactly one has undergone one but not both transformations.

:::

e.g. 1 from T or C ($T \cup C \setminus T \cap C$ ) & 2 from the remaining 261 leaves

$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathbb{P} &= \frac{\begin{pmatrix} 39 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 261 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix}}{\begin{pmatrix} 300 \\ 3 \end{pmatrix}} \\
  &= \frac{39 \cdot 33930}{4455100} \\
  &\approx 0.297 \space_\blacksquare
\end{aligned}
$$

</section>
<section>

### part (c)

:::{.question}

> At least one has undergone at least one transformation.

:::

At least one transformation means 1 of T \cup C (one of $243 + 26 + 13 = 282$ leaves). At least one has at least one transformation means sample space (e.g. 1) minus 0 have any transformations (one of 18 leaves).

$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathbb{P}(\text{at least one}) &= 1 - \mathbb{P}(\text{none}) \\
                       &= 1 - \frac{\begin{pmatrix} 18 \\ 3 \end{pmatrix}}{\begin{pmatrix} 300 \\ 3 \end{pmatrix}} \\
                       &= 1 - \frac{816}{4455100} \\
                       &\approx 1 - 0.000183 \\
\mathbb{P}(\text{at least one}) &= 0.999817 \space_\blacksquare
\end{aligned}
$$

</section>
</section>
