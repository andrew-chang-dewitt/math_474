## &sect; 3.2: counting techniques

at the end of the [last lecture](../0123-ch_3-probability/), we
examined one way to represent a sample space as a tree diagram.
this can be generalized to help count other things:

### multiplication rule

_**def**_&mdash;if an operation consists of `k` steps, & there are `n`
ways to complete step i, the total number of ways to complete the
operation is

$$
\begin{align}
\because
  &&   x &\coloneqq \text{set composed of n ways to complete k steps} \notag \\
\therefore
  && |x| &= \prod^k_{i\coloneqq1} n_i \\
  &&     &= n_1 \cdot n_2 \cdot ... \cdot n_k \notag \\
\end{align}
$$

### permutations

:::

_**def**_&mdash;given a set, `S`, a _**permutation**_ of elements of
`S` is an _ordered sequence_ of the elements. e.g. if $S \coloneqq \{ a,
b, c \}$, then `abc`, `bac`, `bca`, & `cba` are permutations of `S`.

> [!NOTE]
>
> remember, permutations care about order of the rearranged elements!
> two arrangements of the same elements in a different order are
> considered unique permutations!

the total num of permutations for some set of `n` elements is:

$$
\begin{align}
\because
  &&    S &\coloneqq \text{some set} \notag \\
\notag \\
  &&   S' &\coloneqq \text{set of all permutations of $S$} \notag \\
\therefore
  && |S'| &= n!, \text{ where } \quad n \coloneqq |S| \\
  &&      &= n \cdot (n - 1) \cdot (n - 2) \cdot ... \cdot 2 \cdot 1 \notag \\
\end{align}
$$

sometimes we are interested in the number of permutations of some
subset of a set. in other words, the number of permutations of subsets
of `r` elements selected from a set of `n` elements is:

$$
\begin{align}
P^n_r &= n \cdot (n - 1) \cdot (n - 2) \cdot ... \cdot (n - r + 1) \notag \\
      &= \frac{n!}{(n-r)!}
\end{align}
$$

:::

> [!ASIDE]
>
> an example:
>
> :::{.question}
>
> > if you have 8 different locations on a circuit board in which a
> > component can be placed & you have 4 components to place, how many
> > designs are possible?
>
> :::
>
> using $P^n_r \space (3)$ from above:
>
> $$
> \begin{aligned}
> \because
>   && S &\coloneqq \text{set of possible locations} \\
>   && n &\coloneqq |S| = 8 \\
>   && r &\coloneqq 4 && \textit{number of components to place} \\
> \\
>   && T &\coloneqq \text{set of possible designs} \\
> \therefore
>   && |T| &= P^n_r = P^8_4 \\
>   &&     &= \frac{8!}{(8-4)!} = \frac{8!}{4!} \\
>   &&     &= \frac{8 \cdot 7 \cdot 6 \cdot 5}{1} && \textit{factor \& simplify}\\
>   && |T| &= 1680 \space_\blacksquare
> \end{aligned}
> $$

#### permutations of non-distinct sets

thus far, we've been counting permutations of sets of _distinct_ elements; however, sometimes not all elements of the set are unique. in this case, we can separate our set into subsets, each containing all matching elements. if we have a set of `n` elements, of which $n_1$ are one type, $n_2$ are another, & so on until we have `r` groups, then we can find the total number of permutations for those `n` elements via:

$$
\begin{align}
\frac{n!}{n_1! n_2! ... n_r!}, \text{ where } n_1 + n_2 + ... + n_r \equiv n \\
\end{align}
$$

> [!ASIDE]
>
> an example:
>
> :::{.question}
>
> > how many different letter arrangements can be formed by using the
> > letters "STATISTICS"?
>
> :::
>
> $$
> \begin{aligned}
> \because
>   &&   x &\coloneqq \{ S, T, A, T, I, S, T, I, C, S \} \\
> \\
>   && n_1 &\coloneqq \big|\{ x_i | x_i \in x, x_i \equiv S \}\big| = 3 \\
>   && n_2 &\coloneqq \big|\{ x_i | x_i \in x, x_i \equiv T \}\big| = 3 \\
>   && n_3 &\coloneqq \big|\{ x_i | x_i \in x, x_i \equiv A \}\big| = 1 \\
>   && n_4 &\coloneqq \big|\{ x_i | x_i \in x, x_i \equiv I \}\big| = 2 \\
>   && n_5 &\coloneqq \big|\{ x_i | x_i \in x, x_i \equiv C \}\big| = 1 \\
> \\
>   &&  x' &\coloneqq \text{set of unique permutations of $x$} \\
> \therefore
>   && |x'| &= \frac{|x|!}{n_1! n_2! n_3! n_4! n_5!} \\
>   &&      &= \frac{10!}{3! 3! 2! 1! 1!} \\
>   && |x'| &= 50,400 \space_\blacksquare \\
> \end{aligned}
> $$

### combinations

suppose we don't care about the order of the elements we select from a
set? in this case, we are building _**combinations**_ of a set's
elements. the number of combinations of `r` elements selected from a

$$
\begin{align}
P^n_r &= n \cdot (n - 1) \cdot (n - 2) \cdot ... \cdot (n - r + 1) \notag \\
C^n_r &=  \frac{P^n_r}{r!} \notag \\
      &= \frac{n!}{r!(n-r)!} \\
\textit{also }&\textit{can written as the binomial } \notag \\
C^n_r &= \begin{pmatrix} n \\
                         r
         \end{pmatrix} \notag
\end{align}
$$

> [!ASIDE]
>
> let's revisit our circuit board example from before, assuming all 4 components we're placing _are identical_:
>
> using $C^n_r \space (5)$ from above:
>
> $$
> \begin{aligned}
> \because
>   && S &\coloneqq \text{set of possible locations} \\
>   && n &\coloneqq |S| = 8 \\
>   && r &\coloneqq 4 && \textit{number of components to place} \\
> \\
>   && T &\coloneqq \text{set of possible designs} \\
> \therefore
>   && |T| &= C^n_r = C^8_4 \\
>   &&     &= \begin{pmatrix} 8 \\ 4 \end{pmatrix} \\
>   &&     &= \frac{8!}{4!(8-4)!} = \frac{8!}{4!4!} \\
>   &&     &= \frac{8 \cdot 7 \cdot 6 \cdot 5}{4 \cdot 3 \cdot 2 \cdot 1} && \textit{factor \& simplify}\\
>   &&     &= \frac{1680}{24} \\
>   && |T| &= 70 \space_\blacksquare
> \end{aligned}
> $$
